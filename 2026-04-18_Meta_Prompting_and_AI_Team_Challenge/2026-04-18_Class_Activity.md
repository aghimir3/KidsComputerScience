# Class Activity: Meta Prompting & AI Team Challenge

**Date:** April 18, 2026
**Duration:** Full class (9:00 AM - 1:00 PM)
**Format:** Group activity with breakout rooms
**Materials Needed:** Screen share, breakout rooms (Zoom/Teams), students on their own devices

---

## Pre-Class Setup

1. **Create breakout rooms** in Zoom/Teams (3-4 students per room)
2. **Prepare the shared prompt** for Round 2A AI Showdown (see below)
3. **Ensure students have accounts** on at least one AI tool:
   - ChatGPT: chat.openai.com (email or Google login)
   - Gemini: gemini.google.com (Google account)
   - Grok: grok.com (email or X account)
4. **Print/share classwork PDF** (one per team, fillable)

## Part 1: Opening + Recap (15 minutes)

### Quick Review Questions (ask students verbally)
- "Who can tell me what RTCF stands for?" (Role, Task, Context, Format)
- "What's a hallucination in AI?" (When AI makes up false info confidently)
- "What's the difference between zero-shot and few-shot?" (Zero = no examples, few = give examples first)
- "What should you ALWAYS do after getting info from AI?" (Fact-check it)

### Transition:
> "Last week you learned how to write prompts. Today, you're going to learn a cheat code -- how to make AI write your prompts FOR you. It's called meta prompting, and it's what professionals use."

---

## Part 2: Meta Prompting Lesson (35 minutes)

### Teaching the Concept (10 minutes)
Walk through slides 4-7 of the presentation:
- What meta prompting is (using AI to write better prompts)
- Technique 1: Ask AI to write your prompt
- Technique 2: Ask AI to improve your prompt
- Technique 3: Ask AI what questions to ask

### Live Demo (25 minutes)

**Demo 1: Ask AI to Write Your Prompt (8 minutes)**
1. Open an AI tool on screen share
2. Type: `"I want to learn about volcanoes for my science class. Write me the best possible prompt to ask an AI about this topic."`
3. Show the prompt AI generates -- point out how it includes RTCF elements automatically
4. Copy the generated prompt and paste it as a new message
5. Compare the result to what you'd get from just typing "tell me about volcanoes"
6. Ask students: "See the difference? The meta prompt gave us a much better starting point!"

**Demo 2: Ask AI to Improve Your Prompt (8 minutes)**
1. Type a deliberately vague prompt: `"Help me study for my math test"`
2. Show the generic response
3. Then type: `"I just wrote this prompt: 'Help me study for my math test.' Make this prompt much better using the RTCF framework. I'm in 8th grade and the test is on quadratic equations."`
4. Show how AI rewrites it into a detailed, structured prompt
5. Run the improved prompt and compare results
6. Ask students: "Which response would actually help you study?"

**Demo 3: Ask AI What to Ask (9 minutes)**
1. Type: `"I'm doing a project about cybersecurity for my computer science class. I don't know where to start. What are the 5 best questions I should ask you about this topic?"`
2. Show the list AI generates
3. Pick one question from the list and ask it
4. Show how the response is focused and useful
5. Say: "When you're stuck and don't even know what to ask -- this is your go-to move."

### Transition to Activity:
> "Now it's YOUR turn. You're going to work in teams and use these meta prompting techniques in real challenges. The best teams will win awards!"

## Part 3: Round 1 -- Study Guide Battle (40 minutes)

### Setup (5 minutes)
1. Assign teams to breakout rooms
2. Remind students of their roles (Prompt Writer, Fact Checker, Note Taker, Idea Generator)
3. Show Slide 12 with the instructions
4. Make sure every team has the classwork PDF open

### In Breakout Rooms (30 minutes)
Teams work on:
1. Picking a topic they're actually studying in school
2. Writing a first prompt, then using meta prompting to improve it
3. Iterating at least 3 times
4. Fact-checking at least 2 claims
5. Documenting everything in the classwork sheet

### Teacher Rotation
- Visit each breakout room for 3-5 minutes
- Check that teams are:
  - Actually using meta prompting (not just typing one prompt)
  - Iterating and improving
  - Documenting their process
  - Everyone is participating
- Offer suggestions: "Have you tried asking AI to improve your prompt?"
- Give a 5-minute warning before time is up

### Wrap-up (5 minutes)
- Bring everyone back to main room
- Quick check-in: "How did it go? Did meta prompting make a difference?"
- Tell them to save their work -- they'll present later

## Part 4: Round 2 -- AI Showdown + Creative Challenge (55 minutes)

### Round 2A: AI Showdown (20 minutes)

**Shared Prompt** (display on screen before sending to breakout rooms):

> "You are a science teacher for 8th graders. Explain the water cycle in detail, including evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection. Use simple language, include 3 fun facts, and end with a quiz question to test understanding."

**Instructions:**
1. Each student in the group runs this EXACT prompt on a different tool:
   - Student 1: ChatGPT (chat.openai.com)
   - Student 2: Gemini (gemini.google.com)
   - Student 3: Grok (grok.com)
   - Student 4: Can use any tool or try a second prompt on one tool
2. Compare responses side-by-side
3. Rate each tool on accuracy (1-5), detail (1-5), style (1-5)
4. Decide as a group: which tool did the best job?

### Round 2B: Creative Challenge (35 minutes)

**Instructions:**
1. Teams choose ONE creative project:
   - Write a short story or poem
   - Create a quiz for younger kids
   - Design a lesson plan on any topic
   - Build a "how-to" guide for a hobby
2. Must use meta prompting + iterate at least 2 times
3. Document their final prompt and result in the classwork sheet

### Teacher Rotation
- Same approach as Round 1
- For the showdown: make sure teams are actually comparing tools, not just using one
- For the creative challenge: encourage creativity and iteration

---

## Part 5: Group Presentations + Voting (20 minutes)

### Presentation Format
- Each team gets 2-3 minutes
- One person shares screen
- Show:
  1. Their best result (study guide or creative project)
  2. Their original prompt vs. final prompt (show the improvement)
  3. What meta prompting technique they used
  4. Something surprising they discovered

### Voting
After all teams present, have students vote (use Zoom poll, Teams poll, or chat):
1. **Best Study Guide** -- Which team's study guide was the most helpful?
2. **Best Creative Project** -- Which creative project was the most impressive?
3. **Best Prompt Strategy** -- Which team showed the biggest improvement from first to final prompt?
4. **Best Teamwork** -- Which team collaborated the best?

### Announce Winners
- Celebrate each winning team
- Highlight what made their work stand out

## Backup Plans

### If a tool is slow/down:
- Switch to another free tool
- Have students share one tool and take turns
- Teacher can demo on screen if all tools are down

### If groups finish early:
- Challenge them to try their prompt on a second AI tool and compare
- Ask them to write a "meta prompt about meta prompting" (go meta!)
- Have them help another team that's stuck

### If groups are struggling:
- Give them a starter topic (e.g., "photosynthesis" or "the American Revolution")
- Provide a starter meta prompt: "I want to learn about [topic]. Write me the best prompt to ask an AI about this."
- Pair struggling teams with a stronger team for a few minutes

### If time runs short:
- Cut Round 2B Creative Challenge to 20 minutes
- Reduce presentation time to 1-2 minutes per team
- Skip voting and just have teacher pick highlights

---

## Teacher Notes

- **Energy is key.** This is a fun, competitive class -- keep the energy up!
- **Meta prompting is new.** Students may default to regular prompting. Actively remind them: "Did you ask AI to improve that prompt?"
- **Breakout room management.** Visit every room at least once per round. Some groups will need a nudge to stay on task.
- **Celebrate iteration.** When presenting, emphasize teams that showed the most improvement from first to final prompt -- not just the best final result.
- **Safety reminder.** Before students open AI tools, remind them: no personal info, no passwords, no addresses.
- **Have fun!** This is the first group activity -- make it memorable so students look forward to more collaborative work.
