# Class Activity: Internet Map Explorer (Instructor-Led)

**Date:** February 7, 2026  
**Topic:** How the Internet Connects Everything (ISPs, Backbones, Cables)  
**Duration:** ~40-50 minutes  
**Format:** Instructor demonstrates via MS Teams screenshare, students follow along remotely

---

## Overview

This is a guided, visual tour of the real physical internet. Students will see where cables run, how networks connect, and why distance affects speed.

**Remote Teaching Tips:**
- Share your browser window (zoom 125-150%)
- Pause at each map so students can read
- Narrate every click for students with lag
- Ask quick check-in questions after each section

---

## Demo 1: The Internet Is Physical (5 minutes)

### What to Show
- Open a simple slide or draw a diagram:

```
Your Device -> Home Router -> ISP -> Backbone -> Data Center -> Back to You
```

### Key Point
> "The internet is not magic. It is cables, routers, and huge networks connected together."

---

## Demo 2: Undersea Cables Map (15 minutes)

### What to Show
1. Go to: `https://www.submarinecablemap.com/`
2. Zoom out to see the whole world
3. Click a few cables (show name, length, landing points)
4. Zoom into North America and highlight cable landing points

### Things to Point Out
- Cables connect continents
- Lots of cables land near big cities
- One cable can carry HUGE amounts of data

**Check-In Question:**
- "Why do you think cable landing points are near big cities?"

### What If a Cable Breaks? (bonus talking point)
- Mention real causes: shark bites, ship anchors, earthquakes, salt water wear
- "Sharks actually bite undersea cables — it really happens!"
- Ask: "So what happens if one breaks? Does the internet go down?"
- Answer: There are 500+ cables, so traffic reroutes through others (redundancy)
- This is a good moment to emphasize that the internet was *designed* to survive failures

---

## Demo 3: Internet Exchange Points (10 minutes)

### What to Show
1. Go to: `https://www.internetexchangemap.com/`
2. Zoom into the United States
3. Click a few IXPs and show their locations

### Key Point
> "An IXP is a meeting place where networks swap traffic to make the internet faster."

**Quick Prompt:**
- "Which cities have lots of IXPs? Why do you think that is?"

---

## Demo 4: CDN Concept (10 minutes)

### What to Show
1. Open a well-known site (e.g., YouTube or a news site)
2. Explain: "This site uses CDNs so videos and images load quickly"
3. Draw a quick picture:

```
Without CDN: You -> Far Away Server
With CDN:    You -> Nearby Cache Server
```

### Key Point
> "CDNs store copies of content closer to you so it loads faster."

### Examples to Mention
- YouTube, Netflix, Disney+, TikTok (videos)
- Spotify (music)
- Fortnite or Roblox updates (game downloads)
- School portals and news sites (images and files)

---

## Demo 5: Trace a ChatGPT Question (5 minutes)

### What to Do
Walk students through what happens when they ask ChatGPT a question, step by step:

```
1. You type a question on your phone or laptop
2. Your router sends it to your ISP
3. Your ISP passes it along the backbone
4. It may cross undersea cables to reach a data center
5. Thousands of GPUs process your question
6. The answer travels all the way back to you!
```

### Key Point
> "All of that happens in about 1-2 seconds. Every piece of infrastructure we talked about today — ISPs, backbones, undersea cables — is involved every time you use AI."

### Quick Prompt
- "Which step do you think takes the longest? Why?"

---

## Wrap-Up: Key Takeaways (5 minutes)

1. The internet is made of real cables and networks
2. Undersea cables connect continents (and sometimes sharks bite them!)
3. If a cable breaks, traffic reroutes — the internet is built to survive
4. IXPs help networks connect faster
5. CDNs make content load quickly by storing it nearby
6. Every ChatGPT question travels through ISPs, backbones, and cables in ~1-2 seconds
